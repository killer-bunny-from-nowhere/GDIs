**Varanus priscus**
I was able to estimate the largest specimen reported by R. Molnar (QM F2942, length 66.5 mm) using a regression based on the database in Conrad et al. (2012) for varanids (N=21; R²=0.967) to convert dorsal vertebrae length to precaudal length.

> Log10(PCL) = 0.99288 x log10(Dorsal Vertebrae Length in mm) + 1.5831

The result is roughly 2471 mm. Due to a lack of a better method, PCL was converted to SVL simply by using the Komodo specimen AMNH R-37908 (SVL=734 mm; PCL=718 mm). The SVL will then be 2526.473 mm. A specimen from Tomańska et al. (2024) has an SVL of 93 cm and a tail length of 107.8 cm, illustrating that the tail is longer than the body (~53.5% of total length).

> [!CAUTION]
> N=21 is not an authoratitve sample size, and should be taken with a grain of salt.

> [!NOTE]
> The biomechanical models of Dick & Clemente (2017) suggest that 100 kg Komodo dragons already push the limits for the size a monitor lizard can attain. Thus, a 5-meter lizard is to be taken with a high amount of skepticism.
